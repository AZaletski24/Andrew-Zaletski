Ultra-Processed Foods Are Not Always the Cheapest Calories

Exploring the relationship between food processing and affordability across major grocery retailers.

AuthorAndrew Zaletski

Introduction

This visualization explores the relationship between food processing and affordability across three major grocery retailers. The primary takeaway is that ultra-processed foods (NOVA 3) do not universally offer the cheapest calories when compared to less processed alternatives. By comparing the distribution of products across Walmart, Target, and Whole Foods, the graphic aims to persuade consumers that less processed foods can remain competitive in cost. A split violin plot was chosen as the primary mark because it allows for direct, side by side comparisons of data densities between two categorical formulations within each retailer.

Data Transformations

To ensure visual effectiveness, specific data transformations were necessary. The raw "price per calorie" variable exhibited an extreme right tail skew, so we applied a log base ten transformation. This normalizes the data for kernel density estimation and prevents visual compression at the lower bounds. We also clipped the top one percent of outliers to focus on the central distributions. To maintain expressiveness and human readability, we bypassed raw logarithmic y-axis labels. Instead, we mapped the axis to display actual dollar amounts on a logarithmic scale. While clipping obscures the absolute magnitude of the most extreme outliers and the violin density smooths out individual products, this design effectively emphasizes the broader central tendencies that dictate consumer purchasing decisions.

Visual Encodings

For our visual encodings, we utilized vertical position on a common scale to encode price and area to encode data density. To distinguish between processing levels, we utilized categorical hue as an encoding channel. The chosen blue and orange palette provides strong preattentive contrast and is inherently colorblind safe, ensuring accessibility without incorrectly implying a sequential magnitude. The split violin format efficiently utilizes horizontal space, maximizing the core data to ink ratio while allowing the viewer to immediately compare the affordability tails and medians of both processing categories.

Design Refinements

To further refine the design and eliminate chart junk, we removed the top and right chart spines and stripped the legend bounding box. The legend was explicitly anchored below the horizontal axis to entirely avoid data occlusion. Finally, we established a strong visual hierarchy by employing bold font weights for the contextual labels and phrasing the title as a single, testable takeaway. This combination of structural clarity and direct narrative guidance ensures that the audience can accurately interpret the affordability distributions without distraction.
